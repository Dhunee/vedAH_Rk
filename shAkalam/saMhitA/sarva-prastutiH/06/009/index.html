A powerfully enigmatic hymn, in which the poet reflects on the craft of poetry, worries about his ability to practice it, and, on having received the revelation of the mysteries from the ritual fire, takes heart and assumes his poetic vocation. In this progression the hymn is very reminiscent of IV.5, also a hymn to Agni in his Vai┼Ыv─Бnara aspect and one in which a poet receives his poetic inspiration from Agni.
The first and last (7) verses establish a ritual context: the fire is being kindled in the darkness just before daybreak; its light, which dispels the physical darkness, will be configured as the metaphorical light of inspiration in the rest of the hymn. Verses 2тАУ3 are responsive verses, with verse 2 couched in the 1st-person singular voice of the poet. He confesses his lack of knowledge of poetic craft, metaphori
cally expressed as weaving. The burden is all the greater in that he feels pressure to surpass his father, whose poetic heir he is. On the basis of the second p─Бda of this verse, many scholars have interpreted the whole hymn as a depiction of a brah modya, a poetic contest among rival poets, but we see no evidence of a formal contest here, simply a poet struggling to find his place as a poet within the bardic tradition.
The response to this verse in verse 3 provides the answer to the poetтАЩs perplex ity: he will learn his craft if he rightly perceives the ritual fire (who is not mentioned by name). This verse not only provides the answer, but serves as an example of the poetтАЩs growing skill, for the last p─Бda of the verse can be read with double applica tion, both to the poet and to Agni. On the one hand, the repetition of тАЬhigherтАЭ and тАЬbelowтАЭ from 2cd suggests that the same father-son pair is referred to as there, and that the poet is asserting that he will indeed obtain the upper hand over his father from the revelation of Agni. +Jamison Brereton Notes

The 1st person speaker, the poet in training, takes over here, with a statement of his ignorance about his own metier. He expresses this ignorance in the metaphor of weaving, a well-known trope for poetic composition that reaches back into IndoEuropean antiquity.

The main support for the brahmodya interpr. is the loc. samar├й, which is almost universally construed with (├б)tam─Бn─Бс╕е in the sense тАЬentering the contestтАЭ (vel sim.: Geldner: тАЬwenn sie in den Wettstreit eintreten,тАЭ Renou: тАЬquand ils marchent dans lтАЩar├иneтАЭ). But this bends the sense of both words. The other occurrence of the medial participle ├бtam─Бna- (II.38.3) does not signal the type of purposeful motion implied by those translations; there are no other middle forms in the RV, only a single active (I.30.4), whose goal-oriented motion can be accounted for by both the voice and the presence of a preverb. Assuming that тИЪat is continued by younger тИЪaс╣н (see EWA, s.v. AT), the usual gloss of the root, тАШwanderтАЩ, is probably accurate. As for samar├б-, it is obviously formed of the same elements (s├бm тИЪс╣Ы lit. тАШcome/move togetherтАЩ) as sam├бraс╣Зa-, which does usually mean тАШcollision, conflictтАЩ (cf. also the hapax denom. samary├бti), and it has a derivative samary├б- that generally refers to the same. But samar├б- itself is found only twice elsewhere, both times in the meaning тАШgathering, confluenceтАЩ with a genitive expressing goods or spoils (VI.47.6 samar├й v├бs┼лn─Бm, X.139.3 samar├й dh├бn─Бn─Бm), a benign assemblage rather than a hostile clashing together. Thus, тАЬentering the contestтАЭ is at best a weakly supported interpr. of samar├й тАЩtam─Бn─Бс╕е; we are free to interpret that phrase differently and, with the supposed rival poet-competitors removed from the passage, to concentrate on the real competition – that between the poet and his father, as set out in the second hemistich of this vs. However, let us first consider the rest of the first half-verse. The poet expresses his ignorance of three things: t├бntum тАж ├│tum тАж y├бс╣Г v├бyanti. Most tr. try to make t├бntum and ├│tum grammatically parallel, either by making them both nouns (e.g., Renou тАЬJe ne connais point la lisse ni la trame тАжтАЭ) or both infinitives (e.g., Thieme тАЬNicht verstehe ich [die F├дden des Aufzugs] zu spannen, nicht [die F├дden des Einschlags] zu weben.тАЭ). This is understandable, since the two terms are identically formed, with full-grade accented root and -tu- suffix. However, this morphological identity conceals a difference in usage. t├бntu- behaves like a straight noun: it has nominative forms; it occurs in the plural; it has adjectives modifying it (e.g., IX.83.2 ┼Ы├│cantaс╕е тАж t├бntavaс╕е, as well as tat├б- тАШstretchedтАЩ several times) and genitives dependent on it (e.g., IX.73.9 с╣Ыt├бsya t├бntuс╕е). By contrast, outside of this hymn ├│tu- is found only in the clear dative infinitives ├│tave (X.130.2) and ├│tava├н (I.164.5, where in fact acc. pl. t├бnt┼лn is construed with it). I therefore think that t├бntum and ├│tum in this passage are non-parallel, just as the third source of ignorance, expressed in a rel.

cl., is not parallel to either of the others. In my view, having three non-parallel objects to the verb v├н j─Бn─Бmi makes the bewilderment stronger: itтАЩs not just three different things the poet doesnтАЩt understand, but three categories of things – which categories of things are expressed by different grammatical categories: a noun, an infinitive, a relative clause (without antecedent). тАЬI do not understand the thread (noun), nor (how) тАЬto weaveтАЭ (infinitive), nor тАЬwhat they weaveтАЭ (rel. cl.). Although

Since, contra the standard tr., I do not believe that the subject of the verb v├бyanti refers to rival poets, I must propose a different subject. Here the alternative possibilities for ├бtam─Бna- and samar├б-, as discussed above, provide the clues, along with a rudimentary understanding (which is all I have) of the weaving process. With the warp threads (t├бntu-) stretched lengthwise on the loom, тАЬwanderingтАЭ is a pleasingly apt description of the way the weft threads go alternately under and over the warp threads proceeding horizontally, and this mingling of warp and weft could easily be characterized as тАЬa meeting/gathering.тАЭ My only uncertainty is the precise identity of the subjects who do the weaving (v├бyanti). As Michael Weiss reminds me, that Vedic India knew such technology is clear from X.130, a cosmogonic hymn whose operative metaphor is weaving and that contains a word plausibly taken to be тАШshuttleтАЩ (t├бsara- in vs. 2).

Moreover, with the contest interpr. banished, the underlying poets need not be guys physically present in the next room, as it were, polishing their verses; they can be any poets in the tradition. Which brings us to the father.

The second hemistich contains two sets of polarized terms: putr├б- / pit├бr- тАШsonтАЩ / тАШfatherтАЩ and par├б- / ├бvara- тАШaboveтАЩ / тАШbelowтАЩ. As noted in the published introduction, despite his professed ignorance of poetic craft, the young poet feels that he must not only equal but surpass his father, to further the poetic lineage. That par├б- can mean not only тАШhigherтАЩ but also тАШfurtherтАЩ, while ├бvara- means both тАШbelowтАЩ and тАШnearerтАЩ, allows the sense of тАЬfurtheringтАЭ the line also to be read in the passage. The father is close by, both to the poet and the present moment, but the poet himself must go farther, in the future, beyond the model of his father, to speak тАЬwhat is to be saidтАЭ (v├бktv─Бni); it is perhaps ironic that the only other occurrence of v├бktva- in the RV outside this hymn is as a genitive pl. dependent on тАШfatherтАЩ: III.26.9 pit├бraс╣Г v├бktv─Бn─Бm тАЬthe father of what is to be said,тАЭ referring to Agni. It is a nice touch in our vs. that because тАШfatherтАЩ is in the instr., it better fits the phonological template of тАШsonтАЩ than the direct cases would: putr├б тАж pitr─Б╠Б.
