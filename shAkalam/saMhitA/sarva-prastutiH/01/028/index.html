This brief and somewhat racy hymn has attracted a great deal of scholarly atten tion. Most scholarly treatments are more or less in agreement with GeldnerтАЩs interpretation, signaled already in his title тАЬDie vereinfachte SomapressungтАЭ (The Simplified Soma-Pressing). In this interpretation the hymn depicts an abbreviated soma-pressing done at home, on the spur of the moment and without formality, by a sacrificer and his wife, using normal household implements, the mortar (ul┼л╠Бkhala) and pestle, to pound the soma stalks. A woman, presumably the wife, is mentioned explicitly once, in a word play that implicitly compares her regular movements in wielding the mortar with rhythmic sexual movement (vs. 3). Despite the weight of scholarly opinion, we consider this literal interpretation quite unlikely, because the precious and prized substance soma, the focus of the most elaborate non-royal rites, can hardly have been used for a slapdash, playful domestic entertainment.
